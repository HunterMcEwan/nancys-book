The handwritten version on book-001 pp 633–634 is therefore the source draft from which the typescript was made. *[Phase 2 enhanced pass: major corrections from high-resolution crops, all confirmed against the typed counterpart on b002/p032. (1) **Identification of the writer.** Phase 1 attributed this memoir to 'Mary Annie Perry FitzSimons' (the compiler's sister, m. Donald McKay Allston). The textual content makes the writer unambiguously the **compiler's mother Minnie**: same anecdotes — the rat birthmark, grandmother's third marriage to Col. Wm. Fuller, the closing line about 'my youngest son Sam' (= Samuel Cashel FitzSimons, who died in early childhood per the p629 pedigree). The b002/p032 typescript explicitly attributes the same text to 'Minnie Perry FitzSimons'. (2) **Street name**: phase 1 read 'Bay Street' — the handwriting is faded but clearly reads **'Bull Street'** (matching the typed copy: 'a house she owned on Bull Street ... North side of Bull, west of Rutledge'). (3) **'two strangers'**, not 'two negroes': the household euphemism for enslaved people to be bought at auction, repeated in the lullaby refrain on b002/p031–032 ('Mas' Dunkin says want to go to Pee-dee... to buy a pair of strangers'). This is a substantial phase-1 misreading. (4) **'rat incident narrated above'**, not 'rat marred narrative above'. (5) **'unnatural abhorrence and terror of rats and mice'**, not 'unnatural i.e. horror and terror'. (6) **'a gentle darling child'**, not 'a great darling child' — matching the typed line 'He was a gentle, darling child'. (7) **'I always imagined my youngest son Sam'**, not 'I always wondered my youngest born Sam'. The grandfather (here 'after my grandfather's death') is **Edward Perry** — not Benjamin Faneuil Dunkin Perry as phase-1 frontmatter had it; Minnie's father, the rat-birthmark child, was **Edward Dunkin Perry**, named for his godfather Judge Benjamin Faneuil Dunkin. The grandmother who remarried three times is **Maria Ann Hedley Mauger Perry Fuller** (m. 1st Mr. Mauger, m. 2nd Edward Perry, m. 3rd Col. Wm. Fuller of Beaufort) — see the family-tree page b001/p624.]*"

*[Continuation from page 633. Top of leaf reads:]*

> ...that when my father was born the outline of a rat sitting up was on his back near his neck — during the years it seemed to be gradually drawn up so that by the time I was old enough to want to see it it was covered by his hair.
>
> After my grandfather's death the family moved back to **Charleston** to their home on **Bull Street** where they lived until my grandmother married for the third time. She married old **Col. Wm. Fuller** of **Beaufort, S.C.**
>
> It seems in these days almost impossible to realize that the two strangers to be bought were men. To us it seemed perfectly natural to buy, and to the strangers to be bought.
>
> I should have said in connection with the rat incident narrated above, that my father had an unnatural abhorrence and terror of rats and mice.
>
> He was a gentle darling child and I always imagined my youngest son, **Sam**, must be like him. My father was full of charm as I remember him — responding to all that was fine in people or things, and with a delightful sense of humor.

*[Page ends mid-narrative; the closing thought continues, but the typed fair copy on book-002 page 032 makes clear this paragraph is itself the end of the memorandum. The "strangers" in the slavery paragraph is the household euphemism for enslaved people purchased at auction — the same word that appears in the rice-plantation lullaby quoted on b002/p031–032: "Mas' Dunkin says want to go to Pee-dee, want to go to Pee-dee to buy a pair of strangers." Minnie's "youngest son Sam" is **Samuel Cashel FitzSimons**, one of three FitzSimons children who died in early childhood (per the p629 pedigree). She projects her own beloved father's qualities onto the lost child.]*
