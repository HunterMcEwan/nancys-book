> good future with the arrival of Dr. Allison."
>
> Dr. Allison, who comes to the diocese after five years at Grace Church in New York City, said, "I've had a marvelous unification life. I spent 15 years as a theological professor and five years as a New York City parish priest. I am happy here and I look to the future with optimism."
>
> He added, "The Christian faith is unique. Human nature does not change, and man needs to hear the word and believe today as much as in the past. The church must return to the basic [Gospel] and stretch all the world in the [name] of God. The new evangelical efforts in this diocese, the changes in the membership and seminaries are taking the world's way and adopting a sort of Bicentenary style oriented in trying to give the people what they want and not what one Christianity's most do.
>
> Queried about the rejection of religion into the present presidential campaign, Bishop Allison said, "I don't see that it has been rejected. It was not brought in as an extraneous matter. The separation of church and state is correct but that doesn't suggest that people including a candidate, cannot be the full persons that they are. Religion should be in the middle of all American life because Americans are basically a religious people. All facets of the religion should be allowed an opportunity to grow."
>
> Asked about a recent assassination attempt that the Episcopal Catholic Church will consider admission of Anglicans into the priesthood, Bishop Allin said that he is not a new ordination and ceremonies individuals who already have approached the Roman Catholics.
>
> He added, "The chief thing that has happened is the person-to-person thing that is going on all over. There is no threat in the Episcopal Church. This does not bear an us."
>
> Regarding evangelism and its apparent growth Bishop Allin said, "Of course the new evangelism. When they are out, we are not so, they have always tried that. We live in a flatter age. The church must be recognized as a community of believers and human beings attempting to conform to the word of God. There must be faith in God and obedience."
>
> Bishop Allin commented on press reports of recent and continuing movements within the Christian faith and urged that the press use 'gracious language' in reporting religious happenings.
>
> "In the broadcast spirit sometimes labels can be libels," he said.
>
> Asked to comment on the Episcopal Church's 1976 decision not to permit the ordination of homosexuals to the priesthood, Bishop Allin said that ordination should not be based on groups but on individuals. In that, he said, restating a statement made at the 1976 convention, "any decision should be made on individual cases rather than categories."
>
> Asked to comment on a similar group calling itself the Anglican Church in North America, Bishop Allin said, "I find it difficult to keep up with the situation but the basic philosophy of the Episcopal Church is to keep its doors open at all times to all sides."
>
> The ordination services will include a procession of more than 50 Episcopal bishops, groups of choirs and other clergy. The event will be tied to ceremonies of the Anglican period. The order of the auditorium will be filled with the ordination service. Because of the anticipated crowd, visitors should come early.
