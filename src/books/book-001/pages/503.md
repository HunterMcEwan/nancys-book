The writer reflects on American society — that 'apptitude' (aptitude) tends to count for everything; that institutional excellence is not admired; that what is wanted is a man or woman who fits smoothly into the machinery rather than an interesting individual; and that the 'uplift' movement, magazine pictures and Sunday-paper illustrations only stimulate the superficial sensations. > It seems to me in America — deplorably an age where there [aptitude] tends to count for everything. Let a man or woman fit in well to some line of services be sufficiently qualified in some respect for carrying on the activities accepted as useful for the business of life (a narrow field is accepted) and the chief end is fulfilled.
>
> Individual excellence is such as not admired. Good taste is more a [moment?] an obstacle to [prosperity?]. And the interest in human life itself — individuality of character, the various kinds of moral force (except that which consists in yelling on or in excited activity for some kind of "uplift") seem to be falling away. Not a whole man or woman, not an interesting man or woman but one that fits smoothly in is what is wanted.
>
> And it all works for their cramming of the imagination. The great increase in means for constant stimulation of the superficial sensations (movie pictures, Sunday papers illustrations, etc.) does that and the very tense activities of the "uplift people" does that. Whose spirit [moves] [lest] arms & legs [haunt?] play? A. I take in a way to be a marked type
