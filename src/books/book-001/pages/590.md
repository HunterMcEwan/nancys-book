> # Golden Glow's banker turned author
>
> In front-line trenches in France during World War I, Frank FitzSimons entertained fellow soldiers with stories of what he would do back in Hendersonville when the war was over.
>
> He'd marry a redheaded woman, raise red-headed children, cows, horses, and chickens and call his place "Golden Glow." Like the boy in the children's story, he was a youth's golden dream.
>
> Frank went home to Hendersonville after the war, married brunette Magdalen Kershaw and tried his hand at several different jobs — teacher, principal, farmer — and even afford to farm, he says. Up until 1948, the only working part of the farm that he supplied was the dairy. He continued the farm "Golden Glow."
>
> That year, State Trust Company in Hendersonville, which later merged with Northwestern Bank, was lacking an experienced banker. They knew nothing about banking. He used to tell people "up-front lonely man" and would be mind doing radio editorials for the farm as well? He hadn't planned his program to take about Henderson County people and places. Before long, news headed FitzSimons over the radio four times a week. The series of items grew, and as time went on, he himself, with stories of pioneers, who built the town, in Hendersonville's modest cabin.
>
> At home, the farm was taking on character with chickens, ponies, dogs, flower catalogues when amid happenings began to play, and then, brought into the lives of the farm "kids" — Frank's six grandchildren. When they were old enough, they would draw with entertaining and gathering tools grew. Some of the FitzSimons' new garden later had 1,500 bushels in oats and corn at scenes of crime, hangings, court house and entered a big order.
>
> Soon the FitzSimons' rose garden had 300 bushes in all colors and varieties. At Christmas, friends would give them rose bushes to set out, and all summer long the FitzSimons would supply their friends with roses for parties and weddings.
>
> The roses brought so many petty invitations their way that Frank finally quit going.
>
> Nine retired after 25 years in banking, Frank is preparing his publish another collection of excerpts from more than 5,000 broadcasts he did over Station WHKP. Like the first volumes, it will be published by the FitzSimons own Golden Glow Publishing Company.
>
> While he's writing, he's turned the rose garden over to Frank, Jr., after explicit instructions that flowers be returned to those bedside, during the past blooming season, every other day three loads of flowers were headed for managers' meetings each Thursday for the officers to take back to their branches.
>
> One might think Frank would run out of material to write, but he that hasn't happened. He is currently 88, has been at the same intent now with entertaining and bringing more glow into the lives of others than a well-lived dream could ever hold.

> ### Warren named Madison branch head
>
> Don Warren has been named executive vice president and head of The Northwestern Bank in Madison.
>
> He attended East Carolina University and graduated from the Louisiana State University School of Banking. An Eden, N.C. native, he joined the bank as a vice president in the Greensboro office in 1972 and became vice president of the Madison branch in 1978.
>
> Warren is a member of the Madison Rotary Club and is co-chairman of the 1979 Madison-Mayodan Heart Fund Drive. He served as the 1978 vice chairman and was the 1977 vice chairman of the Madison Jaycees.
