## Upper-left photograph

A small oval vignetted studio portrait of a young woman with dark hair pinned softly back, wearing a high-necked white blouse with a dark sash or ribbon, looking slightly to her left. Caption in pencil beneath:

> Louisa F.S.

## Lower-left photograph

A full-length studio portrait of a young man standing at attention in a U.S. Navy enlisted-sailor uniform of the World War I period: white middy blouse with dark scarf, white bell-bottom trousers, the round white sailor cap held in his right hand at his side. Caption in pencil beneath:

> John F.S. World War I

## Centre clipping — "10,000 Pension Checks Her Task"

A clipped newspaper feature, accompanied by a photograph of an older woman in glasses bending over papers at a desk, captioned beneath the photograph:

> MISS LOUISE FITZSIMONS

> **10,000 Pension Checks Her Task**
>
> By MARY BRANDON
>
> How would you like the responsibility of seeing that 10,000 pension or assistance checks reach the right people the first of every month?
>
> That is just one of many tasks that must be handled by Miss Louise R. FitzSimons of 16 Lamboll St., as Director of the Division of Field Service, Georgia Department of Public Assistance.
>
> Miss FitzSimons of Atlanta announced she is the Charleston-born woman, who not only [keeps?] charge of 10,000 payments per [month?] in Georgia [pension and welfare?] payments are not only made but properly made — but a great many [more?] tasks beside.
>
> **IS HE APT?**
>
> "There are 10,000 [pension?] who have made claim for the old age pension," Miss FitzSimons replied, "as well as for aid for dependent children, said the [interviewee?].
>
> [Article continues for several short paragraphs describing the structure of the Division of Field Service in Atlanta and Miss FitzSimons's career rising through the Georgia State welfare system.]
>
> **BORROWERS FROM U.S.**
>
> Miss FitzSimons sees "borrowers" from the federal government as a periodical in her [purview?].
>
> Her latest work in Georgia for the State Welfare Department is to …
>
> In 1937 she was [represented?] to the new Department's [need?] [for?] organization in this state, and the family Pension Association was made [her?] [task?]. In 1949 she became active in the federal Pension Department. By the regional Administration and the Public Pension Administration.

[The print is small and badly aged; portions are not confidently legible.]

## Right clipping — "Miss L. R. FitzSimons Heads Georgia Unit"

A clipping from **The News and Courier, Charleston, S.C., Monday Morning, April 28, 1957**, with the masthead strip glued across the top of the page.

Photograph (left of headline): two women in dark suits standing in conversation; the older woman at left is gesturing as she speaks; the younger at right wears spectacles and a striped shirtwaist with a dark jacket. Caption beneath:

> TWO LEADING FIGURES at activities of the Georgia Conference on Social Work gathering here yesterday were chatting in front of the Hotel Francis Marion, where the convention is being held. Miss Lou B. Champernon, Verda Peyton Conference administrative director (left), is talking with Mrs. Hopperton. Miss FitzSimons is president of the conference. (Staff photo by Cobb).

Headline and article (right):

> **Miss L. R. FitzSimons Heads Georgia Unit**
>
> **Former Charlestonian Named to Honor Division of State Welfare Work**
>
> Miss Louise R. FitzSimons, formerly of Charleston, has been named director of the Division of Field Service of the Georgia Department of Public Welfare. Miss FitzSimons is a sister of the late Christopher FitzSimons, a graduate of the College of Charleston, and a former member of the staff of the Charleston department of public welfare …

[The remainder of the article reproduces her career and family background. Print at the inner column is heavily faded.]

## Lower-left handwritten note

Beneath the John F.S. photograph, a very small snapshot of two seated young women (probably an early-20th-century family photograph) is mounted, and a pencilled note in cursive runs across the lower left margin. The hand is hurried; the legible fragments read:

> Dear Ellie — We had been taken yesterday. They are not good but they deliver / me. That in Carolina alone / with Lui[r] for a — that / some taken by myself but / had a [awful] — I did try / to Uncle Wil[lie?]. mo [for?] / Aunt[?] for it to you. — Were they so / good time on not? — [Mindelle?] / We are all well — I don't / They will go home — go I will [send?] / for [good?] time, [Wash?] [I?][?][?] [&?] price

*[Identification: the family chart on page 271 names children of Theodore Stoney FitzSimons (m. Salina McCrady) — "John McCrady, Louisa de Burian, Theodora Lynch." A John and Louisa pair within the FitzSimons cousinage fits the captions here. Miss Louise R. FitzSimons, the subject of the 1957 clippings, is identified in the second clipping as a sister of "the late Christopher FitzSimons" — i.e., one of the next generation in Atlanta. The compiler appears to have grouped these clippings with the older portraits as a record of all known "Louisas" in the family.]*
