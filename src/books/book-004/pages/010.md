*[The two FitzSimons entries on this page raise a question the bound albums do not answer: who was **Charles M. FitzSimons** of Charleston, and was he the same man as **Charles Mark Fitzsimons State Constable**? The two probate inventories were filed in the same Charleston volume about four weeks apart (14 Dec 1836 and 11 Jan 1837), under consecutive entry numbers 25 and 26. The first describes a comfortable Charleston household — chamber, sitting room, dining room, kitchen, ready money, and a $2,925 interest in the firm of **Innis & Co.** (an account "Subject to bad debts & other Contingencies") — administered by an unrelated party, **Robert Brown**. The second records *no* personal property at all, only money owed to a public-office holder by the State of South Carolina and a note from Lewis M. Porter, administered by **Margaret C. Fitzsimons** (the widow). Two readings are possible: (a) these are two separate administrations of the same man — the first by a court-appointed administrator over Charles's private estate, the second by his widow over the state-owed wages from his public office as State Constable; or (b) these are two different Charleston men with similar names dying within weeks of each other. The bound albums make no mention of either Charles, and no record in the FitzSimons family tree on page 026 of book-001 accommodates a Charles of this generation. The connection (or lack of one) to **Christopher FitzSimons the emigrant** (d. 1825), his brother **James Fitzsimons** of book-001/004, or to any other Charleston FitzSimons line of the period is open. The page is independently of interest for naming **Margaret C. Fitzsimons** — a Charleston Fitzsimons widow not otherwise attested in the album — and for the appraisers **Dunbar Paul, James Welsman**, and **Benjamin Smith**, all Charleston merchants of the 1830s.]*
