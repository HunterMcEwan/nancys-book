One of the big excitements that I remember occurring in the nursery — was the morning Mauma Judy brought in a red bird her son had caught. Of course it got loose and flew frantically around the room — where Mauma Judy caught it — bit but [hugged?] hard. She let it go. I usually [Hardly?] [illegible] [a came?] up and caught the poor frantic thing.

Ellen was our cook. Mauma Judy was her sister. She came every morning and did the upstairs work — and then took the soiled baby clothes home to launder. Mauma said that if a nurse washed and ironed for a baby — then you ruined the baby — and in those days a baby was nursed — held and rocked — and rocked. The big fire constantly going — with a high fender in front of it. A steaming tea kettle on one end of the hearth — and a brown stone tea pot on the other. A low rocker — without arms — and a plaid — innumerable [woolen?] [Mauma walked?] there rocking a baby — home. The nurse drank tea constantly — and it brewed on the hearth all day. Some times — as a great reward — we were allowed a sip. May be that is why I like my tea very strong today.
