supervising the making of the sausage. Two colored women cut up the meat and shabby Seville ground it. Two women cut up the fat for lard. That was done in the kitchen. When the sausages were made, and rendered and the "hog had cheese" was done. Baskets were packed with sausage — molds of hog's head cheese — spare ribs etc. — and sent by express to relatives and friends in Charleston. There were six or eight baskets to get off.

Dad kept his relatives and friends in Charleston supplied with all kinds of produce. Barrels of sweet potatoes — 100 lb. bag of rice. And he never went to town without — taking a basket filled with game — or fish —

When the time for school came we had a governess. Miss Eulalie Barnwell — a 2nd cousin of [Theodore] Roosevelt — was the 1st. Then Miss Lucielle Johnson — who we loved. I would sit in a big chair with her in the evening — before a huge fire and she would tell the most wonderful stories from pictures made of by the glowing coals. She third — and last governess was Miss Ivan FleSaussure. She was very young and gentle — and we did not mind her very well —

Mr. Andrew Sasson was living at Rock Spring then planting for Uncle Theodore — under Dad's supervision
